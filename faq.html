Understanding dementia and how we can help

Dementia is a decline in cognitive function that is severe enough to interfere with daily activities. It is caused by various underlying conditions that damage brain cells.

Alzheimer's disease is the most common cause, but other types include:

  • Vascular dementia
  • Dementia with Lewy bodies
  • Frontotemporal dementia
  • Mixed dementia

While dementia is currently incurable, early diagnosis can help slow its progression and improve quality of life for both the person with dementia and their carers.

Dementia symptoms can vary, but commonly include:

  1. Memory loss - Especially forgetting recently learned information
  2. Communication difficulties - Trouble finding words or following conversations
  3. Planning problems - Difficulty solving problems or making plans
  4. Loss of familiar task ability - Struggling with routine tasks like cooking or paying bills
  5. Poor judgment - Making unusual decisions about money or personal care
  6. Mood and behavior changes - Depression, anxiety, or personality shifts
  7. Motivation loss - Withdrawing from social activities or hobbies
  8. Spatial awareness issues - Problems judging distances or recognizing colors
  9. Inability to recognize familiar people or objects

If you notice these symptoms in yourself or a loved one, consult a healthcare professional for evaluation.

There are six main categories of dementia:

Alzheimer's Disease

The most common type, accounting for 60-80% of cases. Characterized by plaques and tangles in the brain.

Vascular Dementia

Caused by reduced blood flow to the brain, often following a stroke or series of small strokes.

Dementia with Lewy Bodies (DLB)

Caused by abnormal protein deposits in nerve cells, leading to movement and sleep problems.

Frontotemporal Dementia (FTD)

Affects the frontal and temporal lobes, causing personality and behavior changes.

Mixed Dementia

A combination of two or more types of dementia occurring together.

Young-Onset Dementia

Dementia diagnosed before age 65, which can be any type but presents unique challenges.

Dementia is caused by damage to brain cells that affects their ability to communicate. This damage can result from:

  • Abnormal protein buildup - Such as amyloid plaques and tau tangles in Alzheimer's
  • Reduced blood flow - Damage to blood vessels supplying the brain
  • Genetic mutations - Some forms of dementia run in families
  • Traumatic brain injury - Repeated head injuries can increase risk
  • Other neurological conditions - Parkinson's disease, Huntington's disease, and others

Risk factors include age, family history, cardiovascular disease, diabetes, smoking, excessive alcohol consumption, and lack of physical activity.

While there is no cure for most types of dementia, several treatment approaches can help manage symptoms and improve quality of life:

1. Medications

Cholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine) and memantine can help manage cognitive symptoms temporarily.

2. Non-Pharmacological Interventions

Cognitive stimulation therapy, reminiscence therapy, music therapy, and occupational therapy can help maintain function and wellbeing.

3. Lifestyle Modifications

Regular exercise, healthy diet, adequate sleep, social engagement, and mental stimulation can all help slow progression.

4. Caregiver Support Systems

Support for carers is essential. This includes respite care, support groups, education, and counseling to manage the emotional and physical demands of caregiving.
